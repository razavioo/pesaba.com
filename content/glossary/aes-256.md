## Definition

AES-256 is the 256-bit key variant of the Advanced Encryption Standard. It uses 14 rounds of the AES substitution-permutation network. Whether it is required or appropriate depends on the applicable policy, threat model, implementation, and key-management design.

## How It Works

A 256-bit key provides 2²⁵⁶ possible keys. The best known cryptanalytic attacks against AES-256 — including related-key attacks — require computational effort that remains infeasible with any foreseeable classical computing resources. In practice, AES-256 security reduces to the security of the key management system: a well-implemented AES-256 cipher is broken by stealing keys, not by breaking the algorithm.

## Why It Matters for Network Security

AES-256 is selected in many high-assurance designs, but the algorithm name does not establish FIPS validation, Common Criteria certification, or compliance with a national policy. For sensitive OT or government traffic, compare the complete cryptographic module, mode, key lifecycle, interoperability, and evidence rather than key length alone.
